Take a Moment

In the vastness of the universe and its near infinite galaxies, stars, and planets there are an uncountable number of unimaginable worlds each unique in its existence. Among all these planets there are a very select few that exist in the "Goldilocks zone" where liquid water can form. Even within these planets even fewer are shielded from radiation, much like our own thus, potentially support life as we know it. The earth is an incredible place, cosmically insignificant yet so incredibly unique. The earth carries with it one of the rarest things in the universe, life. While it is widely debated how life began, life on earth has endured for billions of years ever changing and adapting to its environment, eventually leading to you and me.

The possibility of life beyond our earthy borders has fascinated humanity for generations, to eventually learn that we are not alone in the vast emptiness of the universe; the harsh uncaring void of space. However, today we will turn our attention back towards our own planet, our home, and our history. From the moment our planet first formed to the present day, there have been so many fascinating events that have led us to this very moment. The world has a lot for us to learn.

The Timeline of Earth

The Beggining: Hadean Eon

  • The Earth is Born

    the earth first began to form from the debris orbiting a very young star, our star, the sun.

    Marking the begging of the first and oldest of the 4 known geologic eons in earth's history. A hellish landscape entirely unfamiliar to us today, temperatures were extremely high, volcanic activity was rampant and the earth was slowly cooling.

    In this harsh environment the first potential life forms arose, with the first universal common ancestor (FUCA) among them and possibly the Last universal common ancestor (LUCA). These life forms would have been simple single-celled organisms (lacking nuclei), called prokaryotes.

  • Formation of the Mantle

    Proto-Earth begins to settle, with the denser metals separated from the other molten material and sank to the earth's center, resulting in the formation of the core, mantle, and setting up the earth's magnetic field.

  • Creation of the Moon

    Early in this eon, after the formation of the mantle, it is theorized that another smaller protoplanet crashed into the earth ejecting parts of the mantle and crust into space creating what would become the moon.

  • Liquid Water

    While temperatures were likely higher than the boiling point of water, liquid oceans formed due to the atmospheric pressure being ~27 times higher than it is today.

  • The First Universal Common Ancestor (FUCA)

    In this harsh environment the first potential life forms arose, with the first universal common ancestor (FUCA) among them and possibly the Last universal common ancestor (LUCA). These life forms would have been simple single-celled organisms (lacking nuclei), called prokaryotes.

The Archean Eon

  • The Rock Record

    The second of the four known geologic eons in earth’s history. The start of this eon is marked by the beginning of the “rock record”, the age of the oldest known intact rock formations on Earth date back to the start of this period. Erosion and plate tectonics has most likely destroyed all exposed formations predating the start of the Archean, excluding sources such as lunar samples.


    Water World

    This Eon was a world of water, where the Hadean was molten, the Archean was wet. There was continental crust, however, it would remain deep under a world spanning ocean for most of the eon

  • The Eoarchean Era

  • The Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA)

    Possibly when the last universal common ancestor (LUCA) existed, the lifeform that would eventually evolve into all life alive on earth today.

  • The Paleoarchean Era

  • The Fist Supercontinent

    One of the earth's earliest supercontinents, which as when most of earth's continents come together in one mass, formed called Vaalbara. It lasted aproximetly 800 million years

  • Photosynthesis

    The first photosynthetic processes evolved in cyanobacteria making it possible for cells to derive energy from the Sun.

  • The Mesoarchean Era

  • The First Ice Age

    The oldest known glacial period occurred in the late mesoarchean era called the Pongola glaciation, lasting 150 million years.

  • The Neoarchean Era

  • Rising Oxygen

    Oxygen levels in the atmosphere rise due to oxygenic photosynthesis.

  • Life Moves to Land

    Prokaryotic microbes move to land for the first time and the first known macroscopic land life evolves, the Thuchomyces.

  • Supercontinent Kenorland

    Supercontinent Kenorland forms, lasting 600 million years, eventually breaking up and becoming parts of what we know of as america, greenland, scandinavia, australia and more.

The Proterozoic Eon

  • The third, and longest, of the four known geological eons in earth’s history. This era saw life take the first few important steps in evolution towards what we know today.

  • The Paleoproterozoic Era

  • The Great Oxidation Event

    Due to the presence of oxygenic photosynthetic organisms the atmosphere changes drastically in the Great Oxidation Event, or Oxygen Catastrophe, permanently increasing atmospheric oxygen. Going from near zero atmosphereic oxygen to as much as 10% of modern atmospheric levels over 400 million years. This drastic change led to an extinction event argued to be on the level of a mass extinction, possibly the first mass extinction in history.

  • Eucaryotic Cells And The Mitochondria

    It was at this point that another evolutionary milestone was hit, the evolution of eukaryotic cells, where cells gained a nucleus, and from which almost all multicellular life draws its origin, including you and me. This is also around the time where the mitochondria is first introduced, likely through the merging of two types of bacteria.

  • The Mesoproterozoic Era

  • Continents

    By this point the continents had become more constant, with less subduction recycling then into the earth mantle, the continents that existed in the mesoproterozoic are more or less identical to those we have today, just arranged differently.

  • Supercontinents

    This era saw two different supercontinents, the first, Columbia arose during the paleoproterozoic and broke up only to reform into a new supercontinent called Rodinia 250 million years later.

  • Multicellular Life

    Multicellular life makes its first appearance somewhere in the Mesoproterozoic, the exact date is highly debated.

  • Sexual Reproduction

    This is quickly followed by the First known instance of sexual reproduction again increasing the level of complexity in life.

  • The Neoproterozoic Era

  • End of the Precambiran

    The last era of the precambrian, marked the emergence of the first rudimentary animals, aquatic plants, and fungus.

  • Snowball Earth

    The Sturtian glaciation, lasting 57 million years and the subsequent shorter Marinoan glaciation, lasting 20 million years, resulted in something called the Snowball Earth. They were two back to back unprecedented glaciation events that likely froze the entire earth from pole to pole with no liquid surface water.

  • Complex Life

    The first widespread appearance of complex multicellular fauna referred to as the Ediacaran biota appeared during this period but were quickly replaced in the next. They consist of Frond-like structures called charnia, primitive sponge-like creatures, jellyfish like invertebrates, and more.

  • Supercontinent

    The Pantoia supercontinent came and went very quickly only lasting about 60 million years

The Present: Phanerozoic Eon

  • The fourth and current of the four known geological eons in earth’s history. This Eon is marked by an event called the Cambrian Explosion, where life diversified and expanded in an unprecedented fashion, quickly filling new untapped niches in which life thrived, and resulting in a large spike in biodiversity. At the beginning of this period life had yet to make its way onto land.
